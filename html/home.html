Until the 1990s, the only planets we knew existed were in orbit around the Sun. Since 1992, astronomers have discovered thousands of exoplanets: worlds orbiting other stars. Based on the data available to us, researchers believe that there could be hundreds of billions of planets in the Milky Way galaxy alone. Current research on exoplanets takes many forms: developing methods to find new worlds, looking for signs of certain molecules in their atmospheres, and studying newborn planets around young stars. Astronomers are working on the next generation of telescopes to find Earth-like worlds and, possibly, signs that life might exist on other planets. The term comes from the words extrasolar planets and refers to a planet orbiting a star other than the Sun. The first exoplanet was discovered in 1995, when Didier Queloz and Michel Mayor discovered a planet orbiting a main-sequence star. It is known as Dimidium. Translated with DeepL.com (free version)
